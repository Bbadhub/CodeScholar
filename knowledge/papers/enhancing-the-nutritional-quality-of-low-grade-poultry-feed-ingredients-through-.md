## Summary

## Key Contribution

## Problem

The rising costs of high-quality poultry feed ingredients have led to increased interest in low-grade alternatives that often contain anti-nutritional factors.

## Method

**Approach:** Fermentation utilizes microbial inoculants to enzymatically break down complex macromolecules in feed ingredients, enhancing digestibility and nutrient availability. Solid-state and liquid-state fermentation techniques are employed based on the moisture content of the substrates.

**Algorithm:**

1. 1. Select low-grade feed ingredient with high anti-nutritional factors.
2. 2. Choose appropriate fermentation method (SSF or LSF).
3. 3. Inoculate with selected microbial strains (e.g., Lactobacillus, Bacillus).
4. 4. Control fermentation parameters (temperature, pH, duration).
5. 5. Monitor degradation of anti-nutritional factors and nutrient release.
6. 6. Evaluate the nutritional quality of the fermented feed.

**Input:** Low-grade poultry feed ingredients (e.g., rapeseed meal, cottonseed meal) with high anti-nutritional factors.

**Output:** Enhanced nutritional quality feed with reduced anti-nutritional factors and improved digestibility.

**Key Parameters:**

- `fermentation_duration: 24-72 hours`

- `pH: 4-6`
- `inoculum_concentration: 10^3 to 10^7 cells/g`

## Benchmarks

**Tested on:** Various low-grade feed ingredients including rapeseed meal, cottonseed meal, and soybean meal.

**Results:**

- Nutrient digestibility, reduction of anti-nutritional factors (e.g., tannins, trypsin inhibitors, gossypol)

**Compared against:** Unfermented low-grade feed ingredients.

**Improvement:** Up to 96% reduction in gossypol and 89% reduction in trypsin inhibitors.

## Abstract

Cross disciplinary medical research has shown that chronic inflammation is on the rise, and one particular area of the body has been gaining attention. The gut. Gut inflammation has been linked with cardiovascular disease, cancer, diabetes, auto-immune disorders, and even mental health. As researchers explore the gut and its microenvironment to understand how these links work, they are also looking for ways to combat the persistence of inflammation. Fermentation and the probiotic effects of fermented foods have led to promising insights in both fighting inflammation and understanding the intricate molecular and chemical relationships that make the gut work. At the core of these relationships are a wide variety of microbes that are gaining increased attention for their roles in creating and maintaining healthy guts.
